About Me

My name is Elizabeth Gonzalez, and I am a third-year Ph.D. candidate in Astronomy and Astrophysics at Penn State. My research focuses on developing methods to mitigate stellar variability in extreme precision radial velocity measurements, with the goal of improving our ability to detect and characterize exoplanets. I am particularly interested in the ways stellar surface phenomena—such as convection and magnetic activity—impact high-resolution spectra and limit our sensitivity to low-mass planets. My work combines observational data and modeling to better understand these effects and to develop tools that can separate stellar signals from planetary ones. I am a member of the NEID and HPF instrument teams, where I work with high-precision spectroscopic data to advance these efforts. I completed my undergraduate studies at Columbia University, where I gained research experience in exoplanets and star formation. These experiences shaped my interest in observational astronomy and ultimately led me to pursue graduate research in exoplanet detection. Beyond research, I am passionate about science communication and outreach. As a first-generation Latina in STEM, I care deeply about broadening participation in science and supporting students from underrepresented backgrounds. During my time at Penn State, I have been involved in organizing outreach events and working with local schools to make astronomy more accessible to the community.
